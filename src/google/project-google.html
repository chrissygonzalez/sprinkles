Class Cards

Class Cards is my submission for the Google Interaction Design exercise. I chose the Names and Faces prompt, which is to “design an experience to help an educator match faces to names, with the goal of shortening the time needed to reach complete un-aided accuracy.”

Teacher Types

I started work by thinking about teachers and where they teach. Schools can be rich or poor. Students can stay in one classroom all day, or they can move around every period. Classes can be held in school rooms with desks, or they can be hands-on in a lab setting or out in the field. Although there’s a lot of variation, the main differentiator between them seems to be whether teachers stay with one class all day or teach multiple classes in periods.

A single class has a limited number of names and faces to remember, but that can expand quickly with multiple classes. How many students might be in a class, and how many classes might be in a day? I did a quick search and found that 30 students is an average class size, and school days can have up to eight periods. So a teacher with multiple classes might need to learn up to 240 names and faces, in chunks of 30.

Learning Names

Teachers have been learning their students names and faces for a long time, so I did a quick survey of methods they suggest. In a series of articles and blog posts, I gathered seven tips for learning names:

  1. Practice saying names out loud repeatedly.
  2. Relate faces to interests.
  3. Think of funny, rhyming, or alliterative word associations.
  4. Memorize in smaller groupings.
  5. Hear how the student says their name.
  6. Write, dance, or draw names and faces.
  7. Have students sit in the same seats at first.
