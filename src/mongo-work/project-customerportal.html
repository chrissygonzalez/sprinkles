MongoDB Customer Support Portal

MongoDB customers are able to request help online, but the user experience is confusing and only available to accounts with paid support subscriptions. From December 2017 to August 2018, I worked on a redesign of the Customer Support portal to make it more intuitive, address known problems, and provide options for all users.

Background

MongoDB has an incredible support organization full of deeply technical support engineers, but they hadn’t had a dedicated designer until I joined. The Customer Support Portal, where customers request help and get updates on their cases, was functional, but it had a backlog of consistent problems and a long list of feature improvements that needed design attention. The portal had also been built using an older version of the MongoDB design system, which meant it was in need of a visual update.

Competitive Audit

This project, my first introduction to the customer-facing side of our support offering, grew out of a smaller effort to update a small part of the knowledge base. I did a quick survey of the entire Customer Portal as a way to get started, and a few things struck me as odd. First, there was no Help Center or Support Home on the MongoDB marketing site – support was either a paid, signed-in service or else customers had to find MongoDB’s Google Group and Stack Exchange answers on their own. Second, we didn’t try to deflect unnecessary case creation by offering common answers and resources on the support site. Third, searching our JIRA backlog surfaced consistent problems we’d known about for years but hadn’t addressed.

I wanted to make a case for making larger improvements to the Customer Portal, so I looked for examples of great support sites and gathered them into a competitive audit. I tried to focus on companies that sell technical or developer-focused tools, as well as companies known for a great online support experience.

Sketches and diagrams

At first, I spent a lot of time sorting through the existing portal, in order to reorganize it into something that made a little more sense. I like to begin the actual design process by giving form to what I know and sketching how things might work before I get into design software.

First draft

My goals for the portal redesign were to fulfill the knowledge base team’s original requests while also trying to: a) make the site organization more intuitive, b) clarify email list and team management options, c) provide resources for both paid and community customers, d) align visual style with current branding, and e) take a first pass at letting users self-help. This first draft focused on site organization, resources for all, self-help, and the knowledge base.
